Our Deeper Roots

The Wanderer & Our Ancestral Story

The Last Illegal Slave Ship

1858 · Jekyll Island, Georgia

In 1858 — fifty years after the United States had banned the Atlantic slave trade — a vessel called The Wanderer arrived at Jekyll Island's waters carrying approximately 409 enslaved men, women, and children from the Kingdom of Kongo in West Central Africa.

This was an act of defiance against federal law, carried out on the very island where our Hill family ancestors would later build their lives and their legacy. The Federal Government prosecuted those responsible in 1860 — but in the Deep South, not a single person was convicted of the crime.

"The same waters that carried enslaved souls to Jekyll Island's shores would later carry Charlie Hill's rowboat — ferrying the millionaires who would employ our family for generations."

The connection between The Wanderer and our Hill family story is profound. The land our ancestors worked, the beaches they walked — all of it was shaped by this dark and courageous history. Knowing it is part of knowing who we are.

Our Cultural Heritage

The Gullah Geechee People

The Hill family's roots are inseparable from one of America's most extraordinary cultural legacies. The Gullah Geechee Nation is a cultural and ethnic group of the Lowcountry — the coastal areas of South Carolina, Georgia, North Florida, and North Carolina. They are the descendants of enslaved Africans who maintained their traditions, language, and identity across centuries of oppression. They are our people.

Gullah Geechee community · Coastal Georgia
The Sea Islands · Early 1900s
🗣️

A Living Language

The Gullah Geechee people preserved a unique Creole language blending African languages with English — born from the transatlantic slave trade and kept alive through generations. It is one of the few African-based Creole languages in North America.

🧺

Art, Craft & Culture

Sweetgrass basketry, woodcarving, and textile arts are among the gifts Gullah Geechee people gave to American culture — tracing directly to West African traditions brought to these shores through ships like The Wanderer.

🎵

Roots of American Music

The spiritual songs, rhythms, and musical traditions of the Gullah Geechee people laid the foundation for jazz, blues, and gospel music in America — genres that changed the world.

👑

A Nation with Leadership

The Gullah Geechee Nation is recognized by the U.S. government as a national ethnic group, with its own Council of Leaders led by Queen Quet, the Chieftess of the Gullah Geechee Nation.

🌊

Resilience Along the Coast

Despite centuries of slavery, segregation, and displacement, the Gullah Geechee Nation continues to thrive with a strong focus on cultural preservation, education, and community development.

🏝️

The Sea Islands

Jekyll Island, St. Simon's Island, and the surrounding Sea Islands were the heart of Gullah Geechee country. The isolation of these islands allowed African cultural practices to survive nearly intact.

🌿 Our Family, Our Heritage

Aaron Hill was a Raftsman, Boatsman, and Fisherman — trades as old as the Gullah Geechee people themselves. Mary Ann Pickney Hill grew rice, made butter and molasses from sugar cane, raised chickens and cows, and sewed every piece of clothing her children wore. These are not simply personal details — they are the living practices of a Gullah Geechee family holding onto their culture, their skills, and their humanity. When we gather as a family, we carry all of that forward.

Chapter One

Our Origins

Aaron Hill

Born 1838 · Myers Plantation, Georgia

Aaron Hill was a man of remarkable character and quiet power. Tall in stature, he was known throughout the community as "The Community Counselor" — whenever there was a dispute or a neighbor needed guidance, people would send for Aaron.

A skilled Raftsman, Boatsman, and Fisherman, Aaron taught these trades to his sons, ensuring the family always had fresh seafood and a steady income. His reputation grew so great that in 1876, the First Lady of the Myers property, Harriet Myers, asked Aaron to be one of the witnesses to sign her Will.

"If there was a disturbance or someone needed advice, the people would send for Aaron."

Aaron and Mary Ann were married in 1863 — the same year slavery ended — and together built a family that would span generations and shape the history of the Georgia coast.

Our Connection

Jekyll Island & The Hill Legacy

The Jekyll Island Club was a private millionaires' retreat founded in 1886 — purchased for $125,000 by families including the Morgans, Rockefellers, Vanderbilts, and Goulds. Our Hill family were the employees who made that island run. Charlie Hill even rowed the first millionaires to the island before it was purchased. 77% of all island employees were connected to him in some way.

⚓

Charlie's Row

In 1886, Charlie Hill rowed a group of millionaires to Jekyll Island so they could survey it. Shortly after, they purchased the island for $125,000. Charlie became Coachman and Caretaker for the Maurice family — and his call "The wheels are rolling at 4!" became legend.

⛳

Earl Breaks Barriers

Earl Hill used his connections with sports figures and Atlanta preachers to desegregate golf in Georgia. In 1964, Jekyll Island was desegregated. Earl founded the Southeastern Golf Tournament, bringing greats like Lee Elder and Calvin Peete, and hosted the island's first desegregated concert.

📚

Anna Educates

Anna Hill returned from college in Atlanta to teach the island's African American children in the 1930s. Her students had to know every state, capital, river, and mountain — an extraordinary standard of education for children of island laborers.

🏡

Red Row Houses

The millionaires built fully furnished, rent-free single-family homes for Black employees — white with green trim, red tin roofs, giving them the name "Red Row." Nearby stood a church, dairy barn, school, and the Picallo House for socializing.

🤝

A Unique Relationship

The Maurice family invited Charlie and his family to dinners and parties, even leaving them the run of their estate. Sam Denegal, Foreman of the Forest, was so trusted that the millionaires named a road after him and tipped him $500 per year — equivalent to $7,840 today.

🏛️

The 2024 VIP Tour

Barbara Hill Floyd's lifelong wish was to visit Hollybourne Cottage — where her Uncle Charlie worked for 51 years. At the 2024 Reunion, that wish came true: a VIP trolley tour of Jekyll Island was arranged specially for Hill Family employee descendants by the Jekyll Island Museum.

Stories From The Island

The People Who Lived It

Charlie Fisher Hill

Born July 1871 · Patriarch of Jekyll Island Employees

Charlie Fisher Hill was born in July 1871 and became the Patriarch of Jekyll Island Employees. Like his father Aaron Hill, Charlie knew about boating and fishing up and down the Golden Isles. One day in 1886, he was approached by a couple of millionaires to take them to Jekyll Island so they could check it out. He took them by rowboat. Shortly after, they purchased the island — completed in 1888.

In 1891 the Maurice Family of Hollybourne Cottage hired Charlie as Coachman and Caretaker of their home. They built Charlie, his wife Angie Parland Hill, and their daughter Anna a house on the Island near their home. Being the first local employee, Charlie got jobs for most of his family, in-laws, and friends. Amazingly, 77% of the employees on the island were connected to him in some way.

"The Wheels are rolling at 4!" — Charlie's famous call. If they weren't ready, he would leave without them. Mr. Maurice allowed it.

Charlie was so respected amongst the millionaires that he was allowed to attend their church and sit wherever he liked. When the state of Georgia took over the island, Charlie and Angie helped the Maurice daughters pack up for their final winter. The Maurice daughters gave many precious items to Charlie and friends — and vowed to NEVER return to Georgia, not even to fly over the airspace. Charlie Hill and his family moved back to Brunswick in 1942, having served with dignity for 51 years.

Anna Hill — The Educator

Daughter of Charlie Hill · Jekyll Island Schoolhouse, 1930s

Anna Hill grew up on Jekyll Island. When old enough, she moved to Brunswick to pursue her education and eventually attended college in Atlanta. She returned to Jekyll Island in the 1930s to teach the island's African American children, and also worked part time at the Club Laundry for extra money.

By the 1920s, the last home on Red Row had been converted into a schoolhouse serving 10–15 pupils on average. School grades went up to sixth grade, after which children attended boarding school in Brunswick. Anna and other educators provided a thorough and advanced education. One student remembered:

"We would have to know every state, its capital, and every mountain in it. Every river, how it flows, where it flows into what ocean — everything. Like Georgia, we had to know Georgia from A to Z."

When not in school, many students worked odd jobs — serving as caddies, boat launch staff, elevator operators, and more for the millionaire members of the Jekyll Island Club.

Earl Hill — Agent of Change

Brother of Charlie Hill · Civil Rights Pioneer

Earl Hill started as a caddie on Jekyll Island at the age of 9. Over the years, he became a well-known Caddie and Bag Carrier amongst the millionaires at the Jekyll Island Club in the 1920s. He knew the game of golf inside and out — and knew how to use all he had learned. Earl took his knowledge of the sport, his musical resources, and his connections (like NFL legend Jim Brown) to work with Atlanta preachers to desegregate golf in the state of Georgia.

In 1964, the court ruled Jekyll Island be desegregated. Earl Hill created The Southeastern Golf Tournament (also called The Classic), bringing legends like Lee Elder, Calvin Peete, and Jim Thorpe.

Earl also had the first desegregated concert in Jekyll Island's Convention Center — Jerry Butler sang during the Award Ceremony. Later concerts included Wilson Pickett, Otis Redding, Percy Sledge, and many others.

Earl Hill is known as one of the most impactful agents of change during a time when change wasn't easy in the South. His experiences on Jekyll Island as a private millionaires club gave him the know-how to make a lasting difference for African Americans. In his later years, he took guests on night tours of the island, sharing its remarkable stories.

Matilda Denegal Hill & Sam Denegal

Wife of Earl Hill · Foreman of the Forest

Matilda Denegal Hill married Earl Hill. Her father Sam Denegal was known as the Foreman of The Forest — overseeing 25 men in summer and more in winter. Sam was so trusted by the millionaires they named a road after him. He received $500.00 in annual tips (equal to $7,840.00 today), and also owned the only grocery store on the island.

One day a woman from the Rockefeller Cottage's car broke down in front of the store. She saw Matilda and asked if she'd be her housekeeper — Matilda said yes, and worked for her for many years.

When the employee church had a special preacher, the millionaires and their chauffeurs would come and enjoy the service so much they'd put as much as $400 in the offering plate (equal to $6,275.00 today) — far more than preachers on the mainland received.

Janie Bell Maxwell Parland

Interview, 1991

Janie Bell Maxwell remembers Jekyll Island before the millionaires, because her father Shepard Maxwell was born and raised on the island. Her husband Charles Parland was a Jekyll Island Club Employee from 1937–1938, cleaning the home of Frank M. Gould. His Uncle Page Parland had worked for the Goulds before Charles. The Gould home was called Chichota Cottage — now called The Villa Marianna, which houses the Jekyll Island Archives.

Janie Bell lived on the island in 1940, and in 1941 she and Charles had twins: Kenneth and Louise Parland. She remembers the Red Row Houses — built by the millionaires so families wouldn't have to travel back and forth to the mainland.

Janie Bell had a Degree in Education and continued her career as a teacher in Fancy Bluff, Georgia. Her son Kenneth Parland still owns the tools his ancestor Page Parland used on Jekyll Island in the early 1900s.

Page Parland

Caretaker & Landscaper · Gould Family Estate

Page Parland worked for Edwin Gould as Caretaker and Landscaper for Chichota Cottage, Shrady Cottage, and the Gould's Playhouse. Page was highly respected among the Gould Family and the millionaires. His wife Aleatha was employed by several island members to air out their cottages during summer. The Gould family built Page and Aleatha a home on the island, and when the island closed, built Page a nice home in Fancy Bluff, Georgia.

Carrie Maxwell Roberts Robinson

Interview, 1991

Carrie Maxwell Robinson remembers the jobs her family had on Jekyll Island: Housekeepers, Golf Caddies, Grounds Keepers, and Common Laborers. At age 16, after her family home burned down, Carrie quit high school to go to Jekyll Island to get a job — against her Father Shepard Maxwell's wishes.

Carrie's first job was Housekeeping and Maintenance for J.P. Morgan and the Hotel. Most of her Hill family relatives were already working on Jekyll Island. The money she made on the island was far more than Black or white people would make in town, so she worked there until she was 24. In October or November, the millionaires would send someone to Jacksonville to buy gifts for the employees' children — bicycles, clothes, toys — then hold a dance contest with a $50 prize (equal to $812.00 today).

When Carrie was 17, she met her first husband Herbert Roberts, Manager of the Jekyll Island Golf Course. A millionaire gave them a record player with a radio — and when the Joe Louis fights came on, everyone came to their house to listen, as they were the only family with a radio.

When Carrie was 24, Herbert died of a heat stroke. The President of the Jekyll Island Club pleaded with her to return to help with his niece. She formed a fast friendship with the niece and they would ride red Go-Carts around Millionaires Village. Carrie left the island in 1940, just before WWII.

Barbara Hill Floyd

Born December 1935 · Interview, 1991

Barbara Hill Floyd was born December 1935. Her parents were Myers Hill and Matilda Denegal Hill. She lived on Jekyll Island at a very young age and returned in 1959–1962. Barbara remembers the housing for Black employees was called Red Row housing — built by the millionaires, white trimmed in green, fully furnished, single family homes, and rent free. Near that area was a church, dairy barn, school, and a Picallo House where employees would socialize and have parties.

Her grandfather Myers Hill cleared the forest for the roads — and a road in Brunswick, Georgia was named after him. At Christmastime, JP Morgan once gave Barbara a pedal truck that used to belong to his son — one of her favorite Christmas gifts ever.

During the Depression, the millionaires paid employees so well that the Hill family didn't experience the hardships happening on the mainland.

Millionaires and employees had an arrangement: Tuesdays and Fridays, employees could go to the beach at night to gather turtle eggs. The rest of the week, millionaires would shoot wild hogs on the beach at night.

One fateful night in the late 1920s, her Uncle Walter went to the beach on the wrong day. He was bent down digging up turtle eggs when a millionaire named Clark accidentally shot and killed him, mistaking him for a hog. Clark felt very bad about it. This happened before Barbara was born.

When Barbara returned to Jekyll Island in 1959, she worked for the Wanderer Hotel (now the Comfort Inn) — the first person employed as a maid there. She also started the Women's Golf Auxiliary Tournaments, held for 25 consecutive years. When she founded the Hill Family Reunion in 1992, her lifelong wish was for the family to visit Hollybourne Cottage — where her Uncle Charlie worked for 51 years.

W. Ray (Walter) Hill

August 28, 1925 —
From his testimony to the Hill Family Reunion

I entered the world as the son of Mrs. Onnie Miller Hill and Myers Hill, residing in Brunswick, Georgia. My childhood was shared with siblings Earl, Arthur, Charles, and Sister Evelyn. We were reared in a home where both parents instilled the knowledge and Love of God at an early age.

Many family members were employed by the Jekyll Island Club. My father Myers Hill trained hunting dogs and served as a hunting guide. Uncle Charlie Hill was caretaker and superintendent for the Maurice Family Estate. Mr. Page Parland and his wife were caretakers for the Gould Family. My Cousin Anna Hill was the teacher at the Club's private elementary school. The Jekyll Island Club provided a health care plan for all permanent employees and their families.

Although family members worked as servants to the millionaires, seeing them live and enjoy leisure shaped our future lives. I learned to communicate with the rich and famous, to understand their conversations about the stock market, securities, and investments. I learned to read the Wall Street Journal. I finally gained the courage to ask questions — and some of them were very courteous and took time to explain.

1941–1945: Enlisted US Army, serving in Valdez, Alaska with the 97th Engineers Regiment — surveying and constructing the Alcan Highway from Alaska to the borders of Canada. Then on to New Guinea at Oro Bay with the 97th Engineers Medical Infirmary. Honorably discharged December 26, 1945.

Enrolled at Morris Brown College, Atlanta, then City College of New York. After graduation, retained as personal manager for the late Redd Foxx.

1946–2003: Diverse careers including Mortuary Science, Private Investigations, College Administration, Spiritual Counseling, Affordable Homes Development, and Pastoral Ministry.

Author of "God's Grace and Mercy Throughout My Life" (Hill, 2016). AME Church Itinerant Elder and Pastor.

To God Be The Glory! — Affectionately, Uncle Ray

A Family Blessing

Lord God

I give you all the members of our family. You know them all so well and love them all. I ask that you would cover us with a rainbow of hope.

Hope that draws us together to work out our differences.
Hope that helps us to celebrate together and care for one another.
Hope that rides like a banner in our lives and watches over us wherever we go.
Hope that overcomes adversity and gathers strength to overcome.
Hope that guides us and gives us vision for our future alone and together.
Hope filled with love.
Hope that fuels faith.
Hope that breathes peace.

May our family live in you
Underneath your promises.

Amen.
